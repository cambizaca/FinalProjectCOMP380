IMPORTANT LEGISLATION

Privacy Laws in the US

There is not one single law in the United States that protects all types of data privacy. There are some state laws, such as the CCPA, which require businesses to share what data is collected to their consumers, but there is no single national law which requires companies to share the extent and purpose of their data collection. And, although there are some laws that have smaller, more specific protections placed on data privacy, few are particularly relevant to social media. Here are a few laws which exist to protect consumer privacy on social media:

  • The Children’s Online Privacy Protection Rule (COPPA): This law limits collecting data on children who are under thirteen years old. Although this law is comprehensive and is implemented on a national level, many social media sites have a workaround of this law by requiring that all users be over the age of thirteen. So, even if children under the age of thirteen are using social media sites, the fact that these companies state in their policies that their service is only for people who are over thirteen gives them plausible deniability and allows them to collect data on all of their users based on the age that was provided to them when the user signed up. So, while this law is in place, many social media companies are able to work around it.
  • The Federal Trade Commission Act (FTC Act): This act allows the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) to investigate and prosecute companies that violate their own privacy policies. It can also force companies to revise their privacy policies if the FTC finds them to be misleading. It has been used against social media companies before, like in 2010 when it charged Twitter with failing to protect user’s personal information after a data breach. The data breach happened when hackers gained administrative control to Twitter by cracking the administrator password, which was a very weak password made up of lowercase letters and was a common word. So, the FTC argued that Twitter failed to prevent hackers from gaining unauthorized access, thus misleading users in guaranteeing privacy.

    • California, Colorado, and Virginia all have some type of overarching privacy law, although they are of varying degrees of completeness.

  • California’s law, the CCPA, and Colorado’s law, the ColoPA, are more comprehensive as they requires businesses to tell users what information about them is being collected, and gives them the option to opt out of selling their personal information and also allows them to delete personal information as requested.
  • Virginia’s law, the VCDPA, also gives consumers the right to access and delete their data, but it only applies to companies that either control the personal data of at least 100,000 customers or control the data of at least 25,000 customers, but 50% of the revenue from selling the data. So, while it does generally apply to larger companies like social media sites, it does not necessarily work on smaller services.

It is important to know what consumer protection laws exist as, especially in the US, the privacy laws are not very strong. Residents of states without specific privacy laws should be cautious about the data that they share, as they may not be able to freely access their data or deleted it if needed. Understanding privacy laws also helps explain why companies write their privacy policies in the way that they do – most companies do not make promises to secure consumer data, as, if a data breach were to occur, they could be guilty of violating their own privacy policy. The current state of privacy laws in the United States allows businesses to not be transparent, which means that as users of social media and of the internet as a whole, it is important to be knowledgeable about what we are sharing.
